# enterprise.powerhouse.io — Target Market & Audience

---

## Target Industries

Industries where data confidentiality is a non-negotiable operational reality — not a compliance checkbox but the foundation of how the business works.

### Legal
Law firms and in-house legal departments handling privileged communications, contract negotiations, due diligence materials, and litigation strategy. AI adoption is blocked by attorney-client privilege and regulatory obligations around data handling. The value of AI is high (contract review, compliance monitoring, matter management) but the privacy bar is absolute.

### HR
HR departments and people operations teams managing employee PII, compensation data, performance reviews, recruitment pipelines, and disciplinary records. GDPR, local employment law, and internal confidentiality policies make external AI processing a non-starter for most HR data. AI value is clear (screening, policy compliance, workforce analytics) but every record is personally identifiable.

### Procurement
Procurement and supply chain teams handling vendor negotiations, pricing agreements, supplier risk assessments, and strategic sourcing data. Negotiation positions and pricing intelligence are competitive assets — exposure to external AI services is a direct business risk. AI assists with spend analysis, contract management, and supplier evaluation, but raw negotiation data must stay internal.

### Finance
Finance departments, controllers, and treasury teams working with financial forecasts, board-level reporting, audit preparation, expense management, and intercompany transactions. SOX, GAAP/IFRS compliance, and fiduciary obligations require auditable, tamper-proof records. AI drives efficiency (reconciliation, anomaly detection, reporting automation) but financial data demands the highest data residency and audit standards.

## Primary Audience

### CTO / VP of Engineering

The technical decision-maker under dual pressure:

**From above:** The CEO and board are demanding an AI strategy. Competitors are announcing AI initiatives. The cost of being seen as slow is career-level. Budget is available for the right solution.

**From within:** Moving recklessly means data breaches, compliance violations, vendor lock-in, or an expensive implementation that delivers nothing. The CTO needs to move fast *and* not create a disaster.

**What they're evaluating:**
- Can this architecture handle our data sensitivity requirements?
- Does it integrate with what we already have?
- Can my team build on this (TypeScript, React, GraphQL — or is it exotic)?
- What's the vendor risk (will this company exist in 3 years)?
- What does the support model look like?
- Can I demo this to the board?

**What they need from the site:**
- Architectural credibility — proven patterns (event sourcing, CQRS), not marketing language
- Privacy story that can withstand a DPO's scrutiny
- Honest positioning — frontier technology, co-creation partnership, not fake maturity
- A demo that shows real systems running on the stack
- A clear path to BAI for assessment and engagement

**The CTO we're targeting** is not the risk-averse buyer who needs 10 reference clients before moving. It's the one who understands that waiting for the "safe choice" means being late — and who sees an early co-creation partnership as competitive advantage, not risk.

## Secondary Audiences

### CIO / CDO / DPO

The people the CTO needs internal buy-in from. They won't read the full site — they'll receive forwarded sections.

**What they scan for:**
- Data residency and privacy guarantees (DPO)
- Compliance story — SOX, GDPR, HIPAA (compliance team)
- Vendor lock-in risk and exit strategy (CIO)
- Total cost of ownership signals (CDO)

**Sections designed for forwarding:** Privacy & Compliance (section 7), Integration & Data Isolation (section 6), Technical Characteristics (section 10).

### Enterprise Architect

The person doing the deep evaluation before recommending to the CTO. They'll read everything, compare against alternatives, and want to understand exactly how the pieces fit.

**What they need:**
- Architecture details — write path, read path, sync, CQRS separation
- Integration specifics — what connects to what, through what protocols
- Technical characteristics table for their evaluation doc
- Links to Vetra documentation for hands-on assessment

**Sections designed for them:** Technical Characteristics (section 10), component subpages (/clint, /switchboard, /connect, /fusion, /renown), Vetra Docs link.

## Audience We Are NOT Targeting

- **The risk-averse CTO** who requires 500 enterprise logos, Gartner quadrant placement, and 18 months of vendor evaluation before a pilot. This buyer will not adopt frontier infrastructure. They are not our near-term market.
- **Developers** looking for a framework to evaluate. That's vetra.io's audience.
- **Crypto/Web3 native organizations.** That's powerhouse.io and achra.com's audience.
- **General AI curious executives** without a data privacy constraint. Without the privacy driver, the value proposition (private + AI-native) loses its edge over simpler cloud AI solutions.

## Conversion Goal

The enterprise site exists to convince and forward to **bai.powerhouse.io**. Every section, every use case, every CTA funnels toward one outcome: the CTO contacts BAI for an AI readiness assessment. The demo request is the secondary conversion — it builds conviction that leads to the BAI engagement.

The site does not try to close a sale. It builds enough trust and urgency for the CTO to start a conversation.
